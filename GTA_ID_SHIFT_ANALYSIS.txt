GTA identity-shift investigation — what's actually going on
=============================================================
Dataset: CHIRLA seq_026, cam_2 + cam_4, ~7755 frames (~258s @ 30fps)
Ground truth: 9 distinct real people across both cameras (ids 2,3,5,6,7,9,10,11,14)


SHORT VERSION
-------------
It is not "ID shifts" in the sense the CHIRLA `shifts` metric measures (a live
track's global_id flipping to a different real identity while still on
screen) — that metric reads 0 in every run below. The real problem is
FRAGMENTATION: the same real person keeps getting handed a brand-new identity
number instead of being re-linked to their own earlier one. 9 real people
ended up as 17-29 GTA identities depending on configuration, and no amount of
parameter tuning tried here got anywhere close to 9. That's the signal that
this needs an architectural fix (the GAEC-over-GlobalRegistry overlay already
sketched in the working plan), not just better numbers.


WHY THE `shifts` METRIC READS 0 DESPITE VISIBLE CHURN
------------------------------------------------------
Every GTA run logs lines like:

    [GTA] identity split: old_id=6 kept_tids=[2019, 1013] split_off=[(9, [2011])]

— meaning tracklet 2011 was wrongly grouped into identity 6, and a later tick
corrected it, giving it identity 9 instead. That is a real correction, but by
the time it happens, tracklet 2011 has usually already ended (it's not in
anyone's `tracked_stracks` anymore). CHIRLA's `_compute_shifts`
(evaluate_tracking.py:303) only walks the history built by `add_prediction()`,
which is only called for a track while it is CURRENTLY live and on screen
(see the probe callback in ds_global_tracklet_association.py). A correction
applied to an already-closed tracklet never appears in that history, so it
can never register as a "shift" — the metric is structurally blind to this
failure mode, not because nothing is wrong, but because it only watches one
specific way things can go wrong (a live label flipping), and this isn't that.

The `reid_accuracy` and `query_accuracy` metrics are similarly poor witnesses
here, for a different reason: both explicitly exclude "unassigned"
predictions (t_global_id == 0, shown as "?") from scoring
(evaluate_tracking.py:212, :240 — `if g != self.unassigned_id`). A tracklet
that never gets a global_id, or a brand-new tracklet that mints a fresh id
instead of correctly re-joining an existing one, still scores as "correct"
in reid_accuracy — because once minted, its own new id gets its own
self-consistent majority-vote mapping to the right real person and stays
that way. Fragmentation is close to invisible to these three metrics by
construction. The only metric that actually exposes it is:

    (number of GTA identities ever minted) vs (9 real people)


TUNING EXPERIMENTS RUN
-----------------------
Three configurations were run end-to-end against this same clip. In all
three, `shifts=0` and reid_accuracy/query_accuracy look "good" or "excellent"
— exactly the misleading signal described above. The identity count is the
column that actually matters:

  Config | window_frames | min_tracklet_len | mu_gap_sec | sigma_gap_sec | link_threshold | final identities | GTA split events | reid_accuracy | query_accuracy
  -------|---------------|------------------|------------|---------------|----------------|------------------|------------------|----------------|----------------
  A      | 600  (20s)    | 10  (~0.3s)      | 2.0 (def)  | 20.0 (def)    | -6.0 (def)     | 17               | 10               | 0.9693         | 0.9259
  B      | 1800 (60s)    | 30  (1s)         | 2.0 (def)  | 20.0 (def)    | -6.0 (def)     | 28               | 1                | 0.9988         | 1.0000
  C      | 900  (30s)    | 20  (~0.7s)      | 3.0        | 10.0          | -5.0           | 29               | 4                | 0.9992         | 1.0000

  (true answer: 9 identities)

Reading this table straight would suggest B or C are "better" (near-perfect
accuracy, almost no churn). They are not better — they are WORSE at the
actual job (17 -> 28 -> 29 identities, moving further from 9), just less
visibly wrong on metrics that don't penalize minting a new id instead of
reusing an old one. What longer windows / stricter tracklet-length actually
do is exclude more short/borderline tracklets from ever being scored or
attempted at all (fewer, cleaner decisions, each less likely to need
correcting later) — at the cost of also linking fewer genuine same-person
reappearances, so more brand-new identities get minted overall. Config A's
shorter window (more ticks = more chances across the video to reconcile
someone's later reappearance with their earlier tracklet) is directionally
closer to right despite its higher per-tick churn, so it's the one left
configured in ds_global_tracklet_association.py after this pass. It is still
2x over-fragmented (17 vs 9) — a "least bad" choice, not a fix.

Tightening the time prior (Config C: mu_gap_sec=3.0, sigma_gap_sec=10.0
instead of the loose 2.0/20.0 default) did not help and if anything made
fragmentation slightly worse. That's consistent with the earlier finding
that, without real per-camera-pair calibration data, any single global
Gaussian gap prior is a guess — tightening it just changes WHICH plausible
reappearances get penalized, it doesn't add real discriminating signal.


WHY TUNING ALONE ISN'T GOING TO CLOSE THIS GAP
------------------------------------------------
GTA's identity decision is effectively cosine-similarity-by-log-likelihood
plus a mostly-uninformative time term (see the conversation this session
that led to the GAEC rewrite) — there's no per-camera-pair transit-time
calibration, and every tick reconverges the ENTIRE tracklet graph from
scratch. That combination means:

  - A genuinely-same person reappearing after a longer-than-window gap, or
    with even mild appearance drift (lighting, pose, partial occlusion),
    frequently fails to clear the aggregate bar against their own earlier
    tracklet and mints a new identity instead - fragmentation.
  - Occasionally two different people DO clear that bar together (a
    look-alike, or two short/noisy tracklets with too little evidence),
    producing a wrong merge that a later tick's full recompute then
    corrects (the `[GTA] identity split: ...]` log lines) - but usually
    too late to matter, since the affected tracklet has already ended.

No combination of window_frames / min_tracklet_len / sigma_feat / sigma_gap_sec
changes that underlying reality - they only trade which of the two failure
modes (fragmentation vs. wrong-merge-then-correct) is more visible, and by how
much scoring exclusion flatters the metrics. This is exactly why the plan
file's next phase - a GAEC layer reconverging over GlobalRegistry's own
already-cleaned, committed identities (outlier-rejected, occlusion-aware,
confusion-zone-checked) rather than raw per-tick tracklet evidence, with an
asymmetric threshold that makes it hard to overturn an existing commit but
easy to form one - is the real fix being planned, not a further round of
parameter search on the current model.


WHAT TO TAKE AWAY
------------------
1. "shifts=0" in any of these runs does not mean the association is working
   well. It means this specific metric cannot see the failure mode actually
   present (fragmentation of already-closed tracklets), only a different one
   (a live track's label flipping).
2. The only trustworthy number in this whole investigation is the identity
   count vs. the known 9 real people. Every configuration tried landed
   between 1.9x and 3.2x over-fragmented.
3. Config A (window_frames=600, min_tracklet_len=10 - the values currently
   left in ds_global_tracklet_association.py) is kept as the least-bad of the
   three tried, not because it's good.
4. The durable fix is architectural (GAEC-over-GlobalRegistry, already
   spec'd in the working plan file), not further tuning of GTA's current
   from-scratch-every-tick, single-Gaussian-time-prior design.
